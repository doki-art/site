The hard parts.

Profile

I started in mathematics and computer science. That's probably why I'm drawn to the products most designers route around: dense data, several user roles, and people who understand their work far better than any tool does. My job isn't to simplify what they know. It's to make the software keep up with it.

Before this I co-founded a design agency and spent four years building it — fifteen people, the recognition you're meant to chase, Awwwards and all. Then I left to do something harder to measure. Agency work hands the design off and moves on; you almost never see what a decision does six months later. I wanted to stay long enough to be wrong about something and fix it. So for the last six years I've been the only designer on one live B2B product, owning it end to end.

The domains have changed more than the work has. Clinicians ordering oncology diagnostics, credit analysts weighing risk, research teams, fleet managers, people filing government reports. Different fields, same shape of problem: software that has to carry a professional's judgment without getting in the way of it, and without pretending to replace it.

What I want next is another problem worth staying on — something complex enough that getting it right matters, with people who'd rather solve it together than pass it down the line. If that's what you're building, I'd be glad to talk.
